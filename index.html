Overview

Executive Summary

Eurimobility is a European equipment manufacturer in electric mobility. The company supplies lithium-ion battery cells and modules, electronic boards, wiring harnesses and plastic parts for electric vehicles and micro-mobility solutions. Procurement represents nearly 60% of revenue, which makes the purchasing function a major lever of competitiveness and resilience.

In strategic categories like batteries, electronics, and wiring, the purchasing portfolio is heavily exposed to Asia in 2024. Increased traceability, decarbonisation, and ESG control are also required by the European regulatory framework, which includes the EU Battery Regulation, taxonomy, and duty of vigilance. Cost uncertainty is further increased by rising and unstable prices for copper and lithium.

The 2024–2026 roadmap seeks to control TCO through Kraljic segmentation, multi-criteria English reverse auctions, and KPI-driven steering; increase the share of European sourcing on these families to about 70%; and drastically reduce Asian dependence in important categories. The ultimate goal of the process is to create a more robust, independent, and environmentally friendly supply chain without sacrificing cost competitiveness.

Context & portfolio

Context, Procurement Portfolio and Raw Material Volatility

Eurimobility is a mid-size company with headquarters in Lyon and three industrial sites: one assembly site in France, one plant in Slovakia for volume and flexibility, and a site in Vietnam for labor-intensive subassemblies. Its customers are EV OEMs, tier-1 suppliers, fleet operators, and micromobility manufacturers. They demand high standards in terms of quality, traceability, and regulatory compliance.

The following six categories will be the main focus of procurement in 2024: electronic components (€105 M), Li-ion batteries (€80 M), wiring and connectors (€42 M), plastics and chassis (€35 M), logistics (€22 M), and indirect purchases (€28 M). Electronics, batteries, and wiring are at the centre of the strategy because they combine high financial weight with high supply risk. The current dependence and the 2026 target for Asia and Europe by purchasing category are shown in the graphs below.

Detailed data show Asian dependence above 50% on electronic components and close to 45% on batteries, with a target of around 30% and 20% in 2026. On the contrary, it is expected that for the same families, more than 70% will correspond to Europe.

Lithium and copper are two main raw materials. Throughout 2023–2024, due to continued oversupply and falling prices, many of the highest-cost mines were shut down or reduced operations. Demand for electric-vehicle batteries and stationary storage rebounded in 2025, especially in both China and Europe, while some producers have intentionally restricted their spot volumes. Combined, these factors created a tight market for lithium and copper from January to July 2025, resulting in prices for lithium increasing by more than 100%.

Between summer 2025 and now, increasing mining projects ramped up, demand returned to a standard level, and some previously built capacity was brought back into production. Prices have come down somewhat since then but are still significantly higher than 2010s levels. Most outlooks for the next ten years suggest there will be a minimum of tripling global demand for lithium, resulting in structurally high prices with cyclical pressures on those prices. Rather than depend on the “cheap batteries” of the past, Eurimobility must incorporate raw-material risk management into its operations and work with suppliers who can guarantee access to lithium in order for them to compete in the future.

Strategic diagnosis

Strategic Diagnosis: PESTEL, SWOT, Kraljic and PARTS

The PESTEL analysis underlines four main forces. Geopolitical tensions between China and the EU and logistics instability – including the Suez Canal and container shortage – pose growing risks of disruption. Economic factors include high volatility in raw-material and freight costs. Social expectations for sustainable and local supply chains are increasing. Finally, the European legal framework is being tightened with regard to batteries, CO₂ and duty of vigilance.

Eurimobility as a procurement organisation has two sides. On one side, Eurimobility benefits from a developed European manufacturing ecosystem; the use of advanced digital technology (like SAP), BI dashboards, and Jaggaer; and a positive reputation related to product quality, innovation, and an existing QCDP scorecard. On the other hand, Eurimobility continues to be highly reliant upon Asian suppliers (63% of total spend) and continues to have a lot of single-source suppliers (about 30% of product lines), while the company's ESG governance framework is still early in its development and has not yet matured enough to enable sufficient risk modelling.

The strategic components identified are: batteries (high impact and high risk), cables (high leverage, high volume, high potential for standardisation), plastics (bottleneck), and logistics (indirect).

According to the analysis of PARTS, the business becomes an explicit and formal game; the participants are: customers of electric vehicles, suppliers in Asia, suppliers in Europe, regulatory agencies, and departments within the firm. Eurimobility provides value through established industrial competence combined with consistent order volumes. Suppliers in Asia offer low-cost, large-capacity, and suppliers in Europe offer proximity, short lead times, and high ESG ratings.

The existence of regulations such as the EU Battery Regulation, Duty of Vigilance and the new CO₂ limits gives Eurimobility an opportunity to accelerate the process of moving production back to Europe. The task is to establish reverse auctioning, dual sourcing, early participation of suppliers in the design phase, and dissemination of the Sovereignty Roadmap. The scope of operations should continue to expand by including additional European partners for recycling and PCB supply, assembly, and decreasing reliance on a small group of Asian suppliers.

Market signals

NLP Insights and Market Signals

An NLP analysis was conducted on a corpus of articles and reports on batteries, critical raw materials and supply chains. The distribution of sentiment scores is globally neutral to slightly positive, with a non-negligible negative tail linked to shortages, dependence on rare metals and geopolitical risk.

The terms that come up within the global word cloud are supply chain, battery, rare metal, stress due, European supply, Net Zero Industry Act. This confirms that, within market narrative, security of supply in metals and European industrial sovereignty have become core themes, together with battery cost and decarbonisation.

These signals support a strategy that focuses on the strengthening of European suppliers while systematically integrating ESG and CO₂ criteria into procurement decisions.

Strategy 2024–2026

2024–2026 Strategy: Sovereignty Trajectory

The 2024–2026 regional trajectory targets a gradual reduction in Asia's share and a rise in Europe as the primary supply base, while maintaining some diversification.

For electronic parts, the company plans to move a large share of orders away from Chinese and Vietnamese vendors and place it with European firms like TechNova EU, BMK Group and Velec Polska through multi-year contracts and dual-source deals. For batteries, Eurimobility will keep raising volumes with European producers – Northvolt, ACC, Verkor, LFP Europe or Tesvolt – while meeting the battery passport and CO₂ rules.

The wiring and connector plan rests on near-shoring work to Eastern Europe; Cablotech Polska serves as the main partner. For plastics, logistics and indirect items, the aim is to cut the number of suppliers as well as to consolidate orders with regional vendors that offer competitive terms.

Auctions & simulations

Multi-Criteria English Reverse Auctions and Simulations

The Jaggaer reverse auction platform is an English-style reverse auction (multi-criteria scoring) where suppliers compete against one another by lowering their bids over a predetermined timeframe and can see how their bid ranks against others, but they do not know who their fellow bidders are. When the set time has elapsed, or no more bids have been submitted, the auction is over.

The ultimate decision is based on more than just the lowest price. Each supplier is awarded a total score based on four weighted categories: 50% price, 20% quality, 20% ESG, and 10% delivery performance. Suppliers must also meet minimum prerequisites for this auction: capacity to provide the required goods, being based in Europe, or providing a credible plan for reshoring to Europe and complying with regulatory requirements.

This auction format works very well with the three families of products that Eurimobility has targeted (electronic components, cables, and lithium-ion batteries) because of the emphasis placed on quality, ESG, and expedited lead times, which are as important as price. Regulation & KPIs

Regulatory Framework and KPIs

KPIs developed for steering Eurimobility's procurement strategy are in line with the latest European regulatory requirements and notably the EU Battery Regulation (Regulation EU 2023/1542). They reflect the new binding standards concerning carbon footprint reporting, recycling targets, and traceability obligations to ensure that Eurimobility's procurement decisions stand in compliance with the sustainability and circular-economy objectives as set by European legislation.

Key regulatory elements

Battery Passport – mandatory from February 2027. A digital identity linked with every battery greater than 2 kWh, EV batteries, and industrial batteries will be made available via a QR code that is physically attached to the product. Information data shall include origin of raw materials, chemical composition, percentage of recycled content, carbon footprint (CO₂), and usage history in terms of number of charge cycles and state of health. It also gives recommendations on recycling and second-life applications. Its objective is to ensure full traceability, total transparency for consumers and professionals, and rapid acceleration toward a circular battery economy.

CO₂/kWh clauses (mandatory carbon footprint declaration). Regulation EU 2023/1542 prescribes strict sustainability, traceability, and transparency rules. All EV, LMT, and industrial batteries above 2 kWh shall declare CO₂ footprint by means of the PEF methodology for their entire life cycle, from raw-material extraction to end-of-life. Carbon performance classes and maximum thresholds must be respected to allow the product on the EU market.

The implementation timeline is progressive: the carbon-footprint declaration will be mandatory for EV batteries in 2025, will extend to industrial batteries in 2026, and by 2027, the Battery Passport will be compulsory and must integrate verified CO₂ data.

These regulatory demands directly impact Eurimobility's procurement KPIs, which must now include carbon intensity in g CO₂/kWh, the proportion of recycled content, and supplier traceability-compliance rates.

Conclusion

Conclusion

Eurimobility starts from a typical situation for a European equipment manufacturer: deeply integrated into global supply chains but exposed to growing risks on costs, lead times, critical metals and regulation. The proposed strategy combines a structured diagnosis, the use of digital tools (NLP, BI, e-sourcing platform), targeted reshoring of volumes to European suppliers and the deployment of multi-criteria English reverse auctions.

This plan lowers reliance on Asia, shores up the European base, sharpens control over raw material risk (in particular for lithium) and meets the higher demands of customers, regulators and investors on sustainability. By 2026 Eurimobility will run a procurement model that is more sovereign and resilient but also ready for the transition, in step with the European energy and industrial agenda.

The next steps are to finalise framework agreements with new European partners, to turn multi-criteria auctions for standardisable families into routine practice, and to set up a cross-functional “critical materials” committee that will give early warning of future strain cycles on strategic metals.
